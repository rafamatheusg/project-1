Professor: Dr. Michael Torres
Course: CHEM101 - General Chemistry I
Department: Chemistry
University: State University

Review 1:
Torres is a demanding but fair chemistry professor. He expects you to come to class having done the pre-reading and his lectures build on that foundation rather than re-teaching the textbook. CHEM101 with him covers atomic structure, bonding, stoichiometry, thermodynamics, and kinetics. His exams are problem-solving heavy — you need to be able to set up and solve multi-step problems under time pressure.

Review 2:
His exams are the hardest part of the course. He gives 90-minute exams with 8-10 problems, some of which have multiple parts. He provides a periodic table and formula sheet but not a calculator — all arithmetic is designed to work out to clean numbers. If you're getting messy numbers, you made an error. This is actually a useful check. He curves every exam: he sets the highest score as 100 and scales everyone else.

Review 3:
Torres has fantastic office hours — he holds them Monday, Wednesday, Friday 2-4pm in the Chem building room 110. He also has a TA-run help room that's open every weekday 9am-5pm and Sunday afternoons. The help room is invaluable before exams. Recitation sections are mandatory and worth 10% of your grade. The recitation TA matters a lot — some are better than others.

Review 4:
The lab component is separate and graded separately. You register for both lecture and lab together. Lab reports are due one week after each lab and take about 2-3 hours to write properly. There are 10 labs total. He is very strict about lab safety — goggles must be worn at all times and he will ask you to leave if you violate safety rules. Lab grade: 10 reports at 8% each plus participation (20%).

Review 5:
Grade breakdown for lecture: online pre-lecture quizzes 10%, recitation 10%, midterm 1 25%, midterm 2 25%, final 30%. He does not drop any grades. Students who work problem sets from the textbook consistently — not just reading but actually solving problems — do well. Chemistry is a skill, not a subject you can cram. His recommendation: do 10 practice problems every night rather than 70 problems the night before the exam.
